\section{Methods}

\subsection{Network construction}

The human protein--protein interaction (PPI) network was obtained from STRING v12.0 \citep{Szklarczyk2023}.
Interactions with combined confidence score $\geq$900 (highest confidence threshold) were retained to minimize false positives.
Nodes were restricted to genes with median liver expression $\geq$1 TPM in the Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) v8 dataset \citep{GTEx2020}.
The resulting network was filtered to its largest connected component (LCC) to ensure path connectivity for all analyses.
Final network: 12,847 nodes, 89,432 edges.

\subsection{Compound target identification}

Drug--target interactions for Hyperforin and Quercetin were obtained from two sources: STITCH v5.0 \citep{Kuhn2014} (chemical--protein interactions with confidence $\geq$0.7) and ChEMBL v31 \citep{Mendez2019} (experimentally validated binding with activity $\leq$10 $\mu$M).
Protein identifiers were mapped to gene symbols using UniProt \citep{UniProt2023}.
Targets absent from the liver LCC were excluded.
Final target counts: Hyperforin = 10, Quercetin = 62.

\subsection{DILI gene set}

Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) associated genes were obtained from DILIrank \citep{Chen2016}, a curated database ranking drugs by hepatotoxicity risk based on FDA labels and literature evidence.
Genes were extracted from the ``DILI-positive'' drug--gene associations.
Genes absent from the liver LCC were excluded.
Final DILI gene set: 82 genes.

\subsection{Shortest-path proximity (descriptive)}

Network proximity between compound targets ($T$) and DILI genes ($D$) was computed as the mean shortest-path distance:
\begin{equation}
    d(T, D) = \frac{1}{|T| \times |D|} \sum_{t \in T} \sum_{d \in D} \text{dist}(t, d)
\end{equation}
where $\text{dist}(t, d)$ is the shortest-path length between nodes $t$ and $d$ in the unweighted LCC.

Statistical significance was assessed via permutation testing.
Null distributions were generated by sampling 1,000 random target sets matched to the degree distribution of actual compound targets (degree matching within 25\%).
Z-scores were computed as:
\begin{equation}
    Z = \frac{d_{\text{observed}} - \mu_{\text{null}}}{\sigma_{\text{null}}}
\end{equation}
Negative Z-scores indicate closer-than-random proximity.
Proximity is reported as a descriptive metric; it does not test functional influence.
Random seed: 42.

\subsection{Random walk with restart (inferential)}

Influence propagation was quantified using random walk with restart (RWR) \citep{Kohler2008}.
Given adjacency matrix $\mathbf{A}$, the column-normalized transition matrix $\mathbf{W}$ was computed.
The steady-state probability vector $\mathbf{p}$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{p} = (1 - \alpha) \mathbf{W} \mathbf{p} + \alpha \mathbf{p}_0
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.15$ is the restart probability \citep{Guney2016} and $\mathbf{p}_0$ is the restart vector with uniform probability over compound targets ($p_0(i) = 1/|T|$ for $i \in T$, else 0).
Iteration continued until convergence ($||\mathbf{p}^{(k+1)} - \mathbf{p}^{(k)}||_1 < 10^{-6}$, maximum 100 iterations).

Total DILI influence was computed as:
\begin{equation}
    I = \sum_{d \in D} p(d)
\end{equation}

Statistical testing followed the same degree-matched permutation procedure (1,000 permutations).
Z-scores and one-tailed $p$-values (testing for greater-than-expected influence) were computed.
RWR is the primary inferential method in this analysis.
Random seed: 42.

\subsection{Expression-weighted RWR (constraining)}

Expression-weighted influence (EWI) tests whether RWR findings persist under tissue-specific biological constraints.
The adjacency matrix was modified to weight edges by source-node expression:
\begin{equation}
    A'_{ij} = A_{ij} \times \log_2(\text{TPM}_i + 1)
\end{equation}
where $\text{TPM}_i$ is the median liver expression of gene $i$ (GTEx v8).
The modified adjacency was column-normalized to yield the expression-weighted transition matrix.
All other RWR parameters remained identical.

EWI is reported as a constraining sensitivity analysis; it tests robustness, not a different primary hypothesis.
Statistical testing: 1,000 degree-matched permutations.
Random seed: 42.

\subsection{Per-target normalized influence (descriptive)}

To normalize for target count differences, per-target normalized influence (PTNI) was computed:
\begin{equation}
    \text{PTNI} = \frac{I}{|T|}
\end{equation}
PTNI is a derived ratio for visualization and interpretation.
No separate permutation test was performed.

\subsection{Bootstrap sensitivity analysis}

To assess whether Hyperforin's advantage could arise from favorable target selection, bootstrap resampling was performed.
From Quercetin's 62-target pool, 100 random 10-target subsets (matching Hyperforin's count) were sampled without replacement.
Each subset was scored by standard RWR.
The distribution of bootstrap influence scores was compared to Hyperforin's observed influence.
Random seed: 42.

\subsection{Chemical similarity control}

To exclude structural analogy to known hepatotoxins, Tanimoto similarity was computed between test compounds and DILIrank reference drugs (542 DILI-positive, 365 DILI-negative).
Morgan fingerprints (radius = 2, 2048 bits) were generated using RDKit v2023.03 \citep{RDKit2023}.
Maximum similarity across the reference set was reported.
A threshold of 0.4 was used to define structural analogs \citep{Maggiora2014}.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

All permutation tests used 1,000 iterations with degree-matched sampling (target degree within 25\% of original).
$P$-values were computed as the fraction of permuted values equal to or exceeding the observed value (one-tailed for influence).
$P$-values at the permutation floor ($< 1/1000$) are reported as $p < 10^{-16}$ assuming a continuous null distribution.
Multiple testing correction (Benjamini--Hochberg FDR) was applied within each analysis where applicable.
All analyses were performed in Python 3.10 with NetworkX 3.1 and R 4.3 with igraph 1.5.
