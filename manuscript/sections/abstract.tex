\section*{Abstract}

Polypharmacology complicates attribution of adverse effects to specific constituents in complex herbal extracts. 
\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St.\ John's Wort) contains multiple bioactive compounds with hepatotoxic potential, but the primary liability remains unidentified.
We applied network pharmacology with permutation-based inference to compare two major constituents: Hyperforin (10 targets) and Quercetin (62 targets).

Using a human protein--protein interaction network (STRING v12.0, $\geq$900 confidence) constrained to liver-expressed genes (GTEx v8, TPM $\geq$1), we quantified influence propagation from compound targets to 82 drug-induced liver injury (DILI) genes via random walk with restart.
Despite having six-fold fewer targets, Hyperforin achieves significantly greater DILI influence than Quercetin (Z = +10.3 vs.\ +4.4; $p < 10^{-16}$; $n = 1{,}000$ degree-matched permutations).
This advantage persists under expression-weighted propagation ($\Delta$Z narrows from +5.8 to +3.5 but remains significant, $p < 10^{-8}$).
Per-target normalized influence reveals 22-fold higher efficiency for Hyperforin.
Bootstrap resampling ($n = 100$) confirms no random 10-target sample from Quercetin's pool matches Hyperforin's influence.
Chemical similarity analysis (Tanimoto $<$ 0.4) excludes structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.

Hyperforin, not Quercetin, drives the network-based hepatotoxicity signal through mechanistic specificity rather than target promiscuity.
These findings support prioritizing Hyperforin content in safety-oriented quality control of St.\ John's Wort preparations.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} network pharmacology, hepatotoxicity, \textit{Hypericum perforatum}, Hyperforin, Quercetin, drug-induced liver injury, random walk with restart
