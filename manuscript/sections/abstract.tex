\section*{Abstract}

Polypharmacology complicates the attribution of adverse effects to specific bioactive compounds in complex herbal extracts. 
\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St. John's Wort) contains multiple pharmacologically active constituents, yet its hepatotoxic potential remains incompletely characterized at the mechanistic level. 
Here we apply network pharmacology with rigorous permutation-based inference to disentangle compound-specific contributions to hepatotoxicity risk. 
Using a human protein--protein interaction network constrained to liver-expressed genes (GTEx v8), we quantify influence propagation from compound targets to established drug-induced liver injury (DILI) genes via random walk with restart. 
Despite having six-fold fewer targets (10 vs.\ 62), Hyperforin achieves significantly greater influence on DILI pathways than Quercetin (Z = +10.3 vs.\ +4.4; $p < 10^{-16}$). 
This advantage persists under expression-weighted propagation, with the gap narrowing (\(\Delta\)Z: +5.8 \(\rightarrow\) +3.5) but never reversing. 
Per-target normalized influence (PTNI) reveals Hyperforin operates at 22-fold higher efficiency. 
Bootstrap sensitivity analysis and chemical similarity controls exclude target-count confounding and structural analogy to known hepatotoxins. 
These findings establish Hyperforin as the primary hepatotoxicity liability in \textit{H. perforatum} through mechanistic specificity rather than polypharmacological breadth.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Network pharmacology, hepatotoxicity, \textit{Hypericum perforatum}, Hyperforin, drug-induced liver injury, random walk with restart
