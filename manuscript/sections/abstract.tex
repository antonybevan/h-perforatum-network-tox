\section*{Abstract}

We compared two \textit{Hypericum perforatum} constituents for network-based hepatotoxicity signal: Hyperforin (10 targets) and Quercetin (62 targets).

Quercetin's targets are closer to drug-induced liver injury (DILI) genes (Z = $-$5.4 vs.\ $-$2.6). 
Despite this, Hyperforin achieves greater influence on DILI pathways via random walk with restart (Z = +10.3 vs.\ +4.4; $p < 10^{-16}$). 
Expression-weighted propagation narrows the gap (+5.8 to +3.5) but does not reverse it. 
Per-target normalized influence shows Hyperforin operates at 22-fold higher efficiency. 
Bootstrap resampling confirms no random 10-target sample from Quercetin's pool matches Hyperforin's influence. 
Chemical similarity analysis excludes structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.

Hyperforin, not Quercetin, drives the network-based hepatotoxicity signal. 
The mechanism is target specificity, not target count.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} network pharmacology, hepatotoxicity, \textit{Hypericum perforatum}, Hyperforin, drug-induced liver injury
