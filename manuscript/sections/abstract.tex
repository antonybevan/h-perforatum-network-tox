\section*{Abstract}

Polypharmacology complicates attribution of adverse effects in complex herbal extracts.
\textit{Hypericum perforatum} contains multiple bioactive compounds, but the constituent responsible for hepatotoxicity risk remains unidentified.

We compared Hyperforin (10 targets) and Quercetin (62 targets) using network pharmacology.
Influence propagation from compound targets to 82 drug-induced liver injury genes was quantified via random walk with restart on a liver-expressed protein interaction network (STRING v12.0, $\geq$900; GTEx v8, TPM $\geq$1).

Despite six-fold fewer targets, Hyperforin achieves greater influence than Quercetin (Z = +10.3 vs.\ +4.4; $p < 10^{-16}$).
This advantage persists under expression-weighted propagation ($\Delta$Z: +5.8 $\rightarrow$ +3.5; $p < 10^{-8}$).
Per-target efficiency is 22-fold higher for Hyperforin.
Bootstrap resampling confirms no 10-target sample from Quercetin's pool matches this signal.
Chemical similarity analysis excludes structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.

Hyperforin drives the hepatotoxicity signal through mechanistic specificity, not target count.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} network pharmacology, hepatotoxicity, \textit{Hypericum perforatum}, Hyperforin, drug-induced liver injury
