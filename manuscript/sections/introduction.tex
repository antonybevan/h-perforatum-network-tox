\section{Introduction}

% Paragraph 1: The problem - polypharmacology and herb safety
Herbal medicines present unique challenges for toxicological risk assessment due to their inherent chemical complexity. 
Unlike single-molecule pharmaceuticals, botanical preparations contain dozens to hundreds of bioactive constituents, each potentially interacting with multiple molecular targets. 
This polypharmacological nature complicates the attribution of observed adverse effects to specific compounds, hindering both mechanistic understanding and rational risk mitigation strategies.

% Paragraph 2: The case study - H. perforatum
\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St. John's Wort) exemplifies this challenge. 
Widely used globally for mild-to-moderate depression, it is associated with clinically significant drug interactions via CYP3A4/PXR induction and sporadic reports of hepatotoxicity \citep{WHO2004,EMA2018}. 
The extract contains multiple pharmacologically active classes including naphthodianthrones (hypericin, pseudohypericin), phloroglucinols (Hyperforin), and flavonoids (Quercetin, Rutin). 
While Hyperforin is recognized as the primary driver of drug interactions, the constituent(s) responsible for hepatotoxic potential remain undetermined.

% Paragraph 3: The approach - network pharmacology
Network pharmacology offers a systematic framework for disentangling compound-specific contributions to complex phenotypes. 
By modeling drug--target--disease relationships within human interactome topology, influence propagation algorithms can quantify how strongly a compound's targets connect to disease-associated genes. 
Random walk with restart (RWR) has emerged as a robust method for such analyses, with demonstrated utility in drug repositioning, adverse event prediction, and mechanism elucidation \citep{Guney2016,Cheng2018}.

% Paragraph 4: The gap and the contribution
Previous network analyses of \textit{H. perforatum} have catalogued target interactions but have not rigorously tested whether observed network proximity reflects functional influence exceeding random expectation. 
Here, we address this gap by applying permutation-based inference with degree-matched null models. 
We further extend the analysis with expression-weighted influence propagation using tissue-specific transcriptomic data to assess whether topological findings persist under biologically realistic constraints. 
Our results establish Hyperforin, not Quercetin or other flavonoids, as the primary hepatotoxicity liability through mechanistic specificity rather than target promiscuity.
