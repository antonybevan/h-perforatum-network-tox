\section{Introduction}

% Paragraph 1: What proximity means, why it's attractive, why it's incomplete
Network-based drug safety assessment commonly uses proximity to disease-associated genes as a prioritization criterion, assuming that compounds with more targets or closer network position pose greater risk \citep{Hopkins2008, Barabasi2011, Guney2016, Menche2015}.
This intuition is attractive: proximity is cheap to compute, interpretable, and widely available.
However, proximity is a descriptive metric---it quantifies topological reachability, not functional influence.
When treated inferentially, it conflates distance with downstream perturbation and provides no principled way to distinguish diffuse polypharmacology from high-leverage modulation of regulatory hubs.
Whether proximity-based rankings are stable across network construction parameters, or whether they are confounded by target set size, remains an open question with direct implications for toxicological prioritization.

% Paragraph 2: Why toxicology is a sharp stress test, why H. perforatum is the minimal example
Toxicology provides a sharp stress test for this question because risk attribution demands constituent-level inference, not aggregate description.
\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St.\ John's Wort) offers a controlled comparison: the extract contains two bioactive constituents with contrasting pharmacological profiles---Hyperforin (10 validated targets, potent PXR activator) and Quercetin (62 targets, broad polypharmacology) \citep{Nahrstedt1997, Moore2000, Boots2008}.
Both exhibit hepatic activity, yet conventional network analysis would predict greater risk for Quercetin based on target count and proximity alone.
This system therefore tests whether proximity-based intuition holds under minimal, well-characterized conditions.

% Paragraph 3: What we do differently, what principle we enforce
We explicitly separate descriptive proximity from inferential influence.
Shortest-path proximity is retained for contextual description; functional influence is quantified via random walk--based propagation, which integrates over all paths weighted by transition probability and captures signal amplification through regulatory hubs \citep{Kohler2008}.
To resolve the target-count paradox, we introduce per-target network influence (PTNI), a normalization that reframes polypharmacology as an efficiency problem rather than a coverage problem.
Bootstrap resampling and chemical similarity controls exclude target-count and structural confounding.
This design tests a specific hypothesis: that proximity metrics are inappropriate for inferential prioritization, and that influence-based propagation provides a more stable framework for comparative network toxicology.
