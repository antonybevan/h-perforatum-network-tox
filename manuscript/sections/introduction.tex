\section{Introduction}

Network-based approaches to drug safety use proximity to disease-associated genes as an implicit prioritization criterion, assuming that compounds interacting with many proteins or lying closer in network space pose greater toxicological risk \citep{Hopkins2008, Barabasi2011, Guney2016, Menche2015}.
Proximity is a descriptive metric that provides useful network context; however, when used for prioritization it conflates topological reachability with functional influence and provides no principled way to distinguish diffuse polypharmacology from high-leverage perturbation.
Whether this descriptive metric is appropriate for inferential prioritization---or whether it is confounded when used beyond description---remains an open and testable question.

\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St.\ John's Wort) provides a controlled system to test this assumption.
The extract contains bioactive constituents with contrasting profiles, notably the phloroglucinol Hyperforin and the flavonoid Quercetin \citep{Nahrstedt1997}.
Hyperforin interacts with few targets yet potently activates the pregnane X receptor (PXR), a master regulator of xenobiotic metabolism \citep{Moore2000}.
Quercetin exhibits broad polypharmacology across dozens of proteins \citep{Boots2008}.
Both compounds exhibit hepatic activity, yet the constituent responsible for hepatotoxicity risk remains unresolved, with regulatory assessments and clinical reports offering incomplete attribution \citep{Hammerness2003, Bauer2004, EMA2018}.

Conventional network analysis would predict greater risk for Quercetin based on its larger target set and closer proximity to liver injury--associated genes.
However, such predictions assume that all targets contribute equally and that shortest-path distance adequately captures downstream perturbation.
These assumptions ignore the topology of signal propagation, where influence depends not only on distance but also on traversal through highly connected regulatory nodes.

We explicitly separate descriptive proximity from inferential influence using random walk--based network propagation on a liver-expressed human protein--protein interaction network \citep{Kohler2008, Guney2016}.
Random walk with restart (RWR) quantifies the extent to which perturbations originating at compound targets diffuse to a defined disease module, providing an influence metric not reducible to shortest-path distance.
Expression weighting further constrains propagation to liver-active genes.

To resolve the target-count paradox---where compounds with more targets may exert weaker functional effects---we apply a per-target normalization of influence scores (PTNI = total influence / target count).
PTNI reframes polypharmacology as an efficiency problem: distinguishing compounds that weakly perturb many nodes from those that exert high leverage through few targets.

We apply this framework to compare Hyperforin (10 targets) and Quercetin (62 targets) across multiple tiers: proximity, influence, expression-weighted influence, PTNI, bootstrap sensitivity, and chemical similarity.
This design tests whether proximity is appropriate for inferential prioritization, and whether influence efficiency provides a more robust criterion.
