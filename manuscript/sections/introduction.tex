\section{Introduction}

Herbal preparations contain multiple bioactive compounds. 
When adverse effects occur, attribution to specific constituents is rarely straightforward. 
This complicates mechanistic understanding and rational risk management.

\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St. John's Wort) is a widely used botanical with known drug--drug interaction liability mediated by Hyperforin via CYP3A4/PXR induction. 
Hepatotoxicity has been reported but the responsible constituent remains unidentified. 
The extract contains pharmacologically active naphthodianthrones, phloroglucinols, and flavonoids---each class with distinct target profiles.

Network pharmacology offers a systematic framework for disentangling compound-specific contributions. 
A common approach is to measure network proximity: how close are a compound's targets to disease-associated genes? 
The intuition is that closer targets should mean greater functional connection.

This intuition is incomplete. 
Proximity counts shortest paths but does not weight them by network topology. 
Two compounds equidistant from disease genes may have very different functional influence depending on where their targets sit in the interactome.

We test this systematically. 
Using a human protein--protein interaction network constrained to liver-expressed genes, we compare two \textit{H. perforatum} constituents: Hyperforin (10 targets) and Quercetin (62 targets). 
We quantify proximity, influence, expression-weighted influence, per-target efficiency, and bootstrap sensitivity. 
We also exclude structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.

The analysis addresses one question: which compound shows greater network-based hepatotoxicity signal, and is that signal robust to methodological variation?
