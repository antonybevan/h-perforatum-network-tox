\section{Introduction}

Botanical preparations contain dozens of bioactive constituents, each interacting with multiple molecular targets \citep{Hopkins2008}.
This polypharmacological complexity hinders attribution of adverse effects to specific compounds.

\textit{Hypericum perforatum} (St.\ John's Wort) illustrates the problem.
The extract contains naphthodianthrones, phloroglucinols, and flavonoids with distinct target profiles \citep{Nahrstedt1997}.
Hyperforin mediates CYP3A4 induction via PXR activation \citep{Moore2000, Watkins2001}.
Quercetin inhibits multiple CYP isoforms and modulates hepatic transporters \citep{Williamson2006}.
Which constituent drives hepatotoxicity risk remains unknown \citep{Hammerness2003, EMA2018}.

Network pharmacology quantifies how compound targets connect to disease genes within interactome topology \citep{Hopkins2008, Barabasi2011}.
Random walk with restart propagates influence through protein networks and has proven utility in drug efficacy screening \citep{Guney2016} and adverse event prediction \citep{Duran2021}.

A common assumption is that targets closer to disease genes exert stronger influence---the proximity hypothesis \citep{Menche2015}.
This assumption is testable.
Proximity counts paths; it does not weight them by topology.
Two equidistant compounds may differ substantially in functional influence.

We compare Hyperforin (10 liver-expressed targets) and Quercetin (62 targets) across multiple network metrics: proximity, influence, expression-weighted influence, per-target efficiency, and bootstrap sensitivity.
We also exclude structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.
