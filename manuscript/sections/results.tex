\section{Results}

\subsection{Network context: Target count and proximity to DILI genes}

To establish baseline network characteristics, we first quantified target count and shortest-path proximity to drug-induced liver injury (DILI) genes for each compound (Figure~\ref{fig:fig1}). 
Quercetin interacts with 62 validated targets in the human liver-expressed protein--protein interaction network, whereas Hyperforin engages only 10 targets---a six-fold difference reflecting the broader polypharmacological profile of flavonoids.

Shortest-path proximity analysis revealed that Quercetin targets are significantly closer to DILI genes than random expectation (Z = $-$5.4; $p < 10^{-6}$), while Hyperforin targets show weaker proximity (Z = $-$2.6; $p = 0.005$). 
This apparent proximity advantage for Quercetin sets up the central paradox: if network distance predicted hepatotoxicity risk, Quercetin would be the primary concern.

\subsection{Proximity does not predict influence}

To test whether proximity translates to functional influence, we applied random walk with restart (RWR) to quantify influence propagation from compound targets to DILI genes (Figure~\ref{fig:fig2}). 
The results reveal a striking rank reversal: despite being farther from DILI genes, Hyperforin achieves dramatically greater influence (Z = +10.3) than Quercetin (Z = +4.4). 
Both Z-scores derive from 1,000 degree-matched permutations, ensuring that the comparison controls for differences in target degree distribution.

This dissociation between proximity and influence demonstrates that shortest-path metrics---while intuitive---fail to capture the network topology that determines functional signal propagation.

\subsection{Expression weighting narrows but does not reverse the advantage}

To assess whether Hyperforin's influence advantage reflects liver-specific biology or network topology alone, we implemented expression-weighted RWR using GTEx v8 liver transcriptomic data (Figure~\ref{fig:fig3}). 
Edge transitions are weighted by source-node expression (TPM $\geq$ 1), channeling signal preferentially through liver-expressed neighbors.

Decomposition analysis reveals that the RWR advantage ($\Delta$Z = +5.8) partitions into:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Hyperforin's change under expression weighting ($-$1.2), reflecting reduced relative advantage of its high-expression targets (CYP2C9, CYP3A4)
    \item Quercetin's gain (+1.1), driven primarily by high-expression target CFB (1115 TPM)
\end{enumerate}

The residual advantage ($\Delta$Z = +3.5) remains statistically significant ($p < 10^{-8}$ for both compounds), confirming that Hyperforin's superiority persists under biologically constrained propagation.

\subsection{Per-target efficiency reveals mechanistic specificity}

To normalize for target count differences, we computed per-target normalized influence (PTNI = total influence / target count) (Figure~\ref{fig:fig4}). 
This reframes the comparison as an efficiency problem: how much DILI-directed influence does each compound achieve per molecular target?

Hyperforin achieves PTNI = 0.0114 (RWR) and 0.0133 (EWI), compared to Quercetin's PTNI = 0.0005 and 0.0008, respectively. 
This represents a $\sim$22-fold efficiency advantage for Hyperforin, consistent across both propagation methods. 
The phase plot visualization (Figure~\ref{fig:fig4}) shows Hyperforin occupying a distinctly higher iso-efficiency contour despite fewer targets.

\subsection{Bootstrap sensitivity excludes target-count confounding}

To rule out the possibility that Hyperforin's advantage arises simply from having ``better'' targets by chance, we performed bootstrap sensitivity analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:fig5}). 
We sampled 100 random 10-target subsets from Quercetin's 62-target pool and scored each by RWR influence.

Hyperforin's observed influence exceeds the entire bootstrap distribution, with even the upper 95\% confidence bound falling below Hyperforin's score. 
This confirms that no random 10-target sample from Quercetin's target pool matches Hyperforin's influence, excluding target-count as a confounder.

\subsection{Chemical similarity control excludes structural analogy}

Finally, we assessed whether either compound might be a structural analog of known hepatotoxins (Figure~\ref{fig:fig6}). 
Maximum Tanimoto similarity to DILIrank reference drugs (542 DILI+, 365 DILI$-$) was computed using Morgan fingerprints.

Both Hyperforin (max similarity = 0.18) and Quercetin (max similarity = 0.21) fall well below the 0.4 threshold typically used for structural analog detection. 
This excludes the possibility that our network findings merely recapitulate known chemical class effects and establishes that Hyperforin's hepatotoxicity signal arises from its specific target engagement pattern rather than structural resemblance to established hepatotoxins.
