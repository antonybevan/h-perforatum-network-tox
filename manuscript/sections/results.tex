\section{Results}

\subsection{Target count and proximity provide misleading context}

We quantified network position for each compound's targets relative to drug-induced liver injury (DILI) genes (Figure~\ref{fig:fig1}). 
Quercetin engages 62 validated targets; Hyperforin engages 10. 
Quercetin's targets are closer to DILI genes than expected by chance (Z = $-$5.4; $p < 10^{-6}$); Hyperforin's are marginally closer (Z = $-$2.6; $p = 0.005$). 
These proximity metrics are descriptive and do not test functional influence.

\subsection{Network proximity fails to predict influence}

We tested whether proximity predicts influence on DILI pathways using random walk with restart (Figure~\ref{fig:fig2}). 
Hyperforin achieves higher influence (Z = +10.3) than Quercetin (Z = +4.4), despite being more distant from DILI genes. 
This result does not imply that proximity is useless; it demonstrates that proximity alone is insufficient to predict functional influence.

\subsection{Expression weighting redistributes but does not negate influence}

We tested whether Hyperforin's advantage reflects network topology or liver-specific biology by applying expression-weighted influence propagation (Figure~\ref{fig:fig3}). 
The Z-score gap narrows from +5.8 (RWR) to +3.5 (EWI): Hyperforin loses 1.2 Z-score units; Quercetin gains 1.1 units (driven by CFB at 1115 TPM). 
This does not indicate that expression weighting ``rescues'' Quercetin; the residual gap remains significant ($p < 10^{-8}$).

\subsection{Influence efficiency explains the target-count paradox}

We computed per-target normalized influence (PTNI = total influence / target count) to address whether Hyperforin's advantage arises from target efficiency rather than target count (Figure~\ref{fig:fig4}). 
Hyperforin achieves PTNI = 0.0114 (RWR) and 0.0133 (EWI); Quercetin achieves 0.0005 and 0.0008, respectively---a 22-fold difference. 
PTNI is a derived descriptive metric; no separate permutation test was performed.

\subsection{Bootstrap resampling rules out target-count artefacts}

We tested whether any random 10-target subset from Quercetin's pool could match Hyperforin's influence (Figure~\ref{fig:fig5}). 
From 100 bootstrap samples, none achieved Hyperforin's observed influence; the 95th percentile of the distribution falls below Hyperforin's score. 
This does not prove causation; it excludes target-count as a sufficient explanation.

\subsection{Chemical similarity does not explain the observed signal}

We tested whether either compound is a structural analog of known hepatotoxins (Figure~\ref{fig:fig6}). 
Maximum Tanimoto similarity to DILIrank reference drugs was 0.18 (Hyperforin) and 0.21 (Quercetin), both below the 0.4 analog threshold. 
This does not rule out novel mechanisms; it excludes structural analogy as the explanation for the network signal.
