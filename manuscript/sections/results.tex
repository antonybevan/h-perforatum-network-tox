\section{Results}

\subsection{Network context}

Quercetin engages 62 targets in the liver-expressed network; Hyperforin engages 10 (Figure~\ref{fig:fig1}).
Quercetin targets are closer to DILI genes than random expectation (Z = $-$5.4; $p < 10^{-6}$); Hyperforin targets are marginally closer (Z = $-$2.6; $p = 0.005$).
Proximity is descriptive; it does not test influence.

\subsection{Proximity does not predict influence}

Random walk with restart reveals a rank reversal (Figure~\ref{fig:fig2}).
Hyperforin achieves greater influence than Quercetin (Z = +10.3 vs.\ +4.4; $p < 10^{-16}$; $n = 1{,}000$ degree-matched permutations) despite being topologically more distant.
Shorter paths do not guarantee stronger influence.

\subsection{Expression weighting narrows but does not reverse the gap}

Expression-weighted propagation reduces the Z-score gap from +5.8 to +3.5 (Figure~\ref{fig:fig3}).
Hyperforin loses 1.2 units (CYP2C9, CYP3A4 attenuation); Quercetin gains 1.1 units (CFB at 1{,}115 TPM).
The residual gap remains significant ($p < 10^{-8}$).

\subsection{Per-target efficiency}

Per-target normalized influence (PTNI = influence / targets) reframes the comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:fig4}).
Hyperforin: PTNI = 0.0114 (RWR), 0.0133 (EWI).
Quercetin: PTNI = 0.0005 (RWR), 0.0008 (EWI).
Hyperforin operates at 22-fold higher efficiency.
PTNI is descriptive; no separate permutation test was performed.

\subsection{Bootstrap sensitivity}

We sampled 100 random 10-target subsets from Quercetin's pool (Figure~\ref{fig:fig5}).
Hyperforin exceeds the entire distribution.
Target count does not explain the signal.

\subsection{Chemical similarity}

Maximum Tanimoto similarity to DILIrank drugs: Hyperforin = 0.18, Quercetin = 0.21 (Figure~\ref{fig:fig6}).
Both fall below the 0.4 analog threshold \citep{Maggiora2014}.
Structural resemblance does not explain the signal.
