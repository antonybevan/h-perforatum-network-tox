\section{Results}

\subsection{Quercetin targets are closer to DILI genes but exert weaker influence}

We first established network context by quantifying target count and shortest-path proximity to 82 DILI-associated genes (Figure~\ref{fig:fig1}).
Quercetin engages 62 targets in the liver-expressed largest connected component; Hyperforin engages 10.
Shortest-path proximity reveals that Quercetin targets are significantly closer to DILI genes than degree-matched random expectation (Z = $-$5.4; one-tailed $p < 10^{-6}$; $n = 1{,}000$ permutations; FDR-corrected), while Hyperforin targets exhibit weaker proximity (Z = $-$2.6; $p = 0.005$; FDR-corrected).
By conventional interpretation, Quercetin should pose greater hepatotoxicity risk.

Random walk with restart (RWR) reverses this ranking (Figure~\ref{fig:fig2}).
Hyperforin achieves influence Z = +10.3 (one-tailed $p < 10^{-16}$; at permutation floor); Quercetin achieves Z = +4.4 ($p < 10^{-6}$).
Both are significant; the magnitudes differ by a factor of 2.3 in Z-score space.
Despite being topologically more distant from DILI genes, Hyperforin exerts substantially greater network influence.

This dissociation between proximity and influence---the proximity paradox---demonstrates that shortest-path distance is not a reliable surrogate for functional impact.

\subsection{Expression weighting attenuates but does not reverse the advantage}

To assess whether the RWR signal persists under tissue-specific biological constraint, we applied expression-weighted influence propagation (EWI), weighting network transitions by liver gene expression (Figure~\ref{fig:fig3}).

The Z-score differential narrows under expression weighting: $\Delta$Z = +5.8 (RWR) $\rightarrow$ +3.5 (EWI).
Decomposition reveals the mechanism: Hyperforin loses 1.2 Z-score units due to attenuation of high-expression targets (CYP2C9, CYP3A4), while Quercetin gains 1.1 units, driven primarily by complement factor B (CFB) at 1{,}115 TPM.

The residual gap remains highly significant: Hyperforin Z = +9.1 (one-tailed $p < 10^{-16}$; at permutation floor); Quercetin Z = +5.6 ($p < 10^{-8}$; FDR-corrected).
Expression weighting did not manufacture the signal; it refined it.
Attenuation is expected when propagation is constrained to biologically active nodes; it does not constitute invalidation.

\subsection{Per-target efficiency reveals a 22-fold disparity}

To resolve the target-count paradox---where compounds with more targets may exert weaker functional effects---we computed per-target normalized influence (PTNI = total influence / target count), reframing polypharmacology as an efficiency problem rather than a coverage problem (Figure~\ref{fig:fig4}).

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Compound} & \textbf{Targets} & \textbf{PTNI (RWR)} & \textbf{PTNI (EWI)} & \textbf{Ratio} \\
\midrule
Hyperforin & 10 & 0.0114 & 0.0133 & --- \\
Quercetin & 62 & 0.0005 & 0.0008 & --- \\
\textbf{Fold difference} & --- & 22$\times$ & 17$\times$ & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each Hyperforin target contributes 17--22$\times$ more DILI-directed influence than each Quercetin target.
This disparity is consistent across both topology-only (RWR) and expression-weighted (EWI) analyses, indicating that the efficiency advantage is robust to biological constraint.

PTNI is a derived descriptive metric; it was not subjected to independent permutation testing.
Its utility lies in normalizing for target count, not in providing additional inference.

\subsection{Bootstrap resampling excludes target-count confounding}

To rule out the possibility that Hyperforin's advantage arises from favorable target selection rather than strategic network positioning, we performed bootstrap sensitivity analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:fig5}).
100 random 10-target subsets were sampled without replacement from Quercetin's 62-target pool and scored by RWR (random seed 42 for reproducibility).

Hyperforin's observed influence (0.114) exceeds the entire bootstrap distribution.
The 95th percentile of the Quercetin bootstrap (0.054) falls below Hyperforin's score.
The fold difference between Hyperforin and the bootstrap mean is 3.7$\times$.

This confirms that Hyperforin's advantage is not an artifact of target count.
Even when sampling equalized subsets from Quercetin's pool, no configuration matches Hyperforin's influence.
Bootstrap analysis is a robustness control; it strengthens confidence in the ranking without providing independent evidence.

\subsection{Threshold robustness}

The influence ranking is stable across network confidence thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:threshold_robustness}).
At STRING confidence $\geq$700 (11{,}693 nodes, 100{,}383 edges), Hyperforin achieves RWR Z = +10.9 and EWI Z = +10.5; at $\geq$900 (7{,}677 nodes, 66{,}908 edges), Z = +10.3 and +9.1 respectively.
Hyperforin ranks first in all configurations.

Notably, the proximity ranking reverses between thresholds: at $\geq$700, Hyperforin is closer to DILI genes (Z = $-$6.04) than Quercetin (Z = $-$5.46); at $\geq$900, Quercetin is closer (Z = $-$5.44 vs $-$3.86).
This instability in proximity rankings---while influence rankings remain stable---demonstrates that influence-based metrics are more robust to network construction parameters than shortest-path distance.

\subsection{Chemical similarity excludes structural confounding}

To exclude the possibility that Hyperforin's network signal reflects structural similarity to known hepatotoxins, we performed chemical similarity analysis against the DILIrank reference set \\citep{Chen2016} (Figure~\\ref{fig:fig6}).
Morgan fingerprints (ECFP4, radius 2, 2048 bits) were computed for Hyperforin and Quercetin, and maximum Tanimoto similarity to 542 DILI-positive and 365 DILI-negative drugs was determined.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Compound} & \textbf{Max (DILI+)} & \textbf{Max (DILI$-$)} & \textbf{Analog?} \\
\midrule
Hyperforin & 0.15 & 0.20 & No \\
Quercetin & 0.21 & 0.22 & No \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Neither compound exceeds the 0.4 threshold for structural analog detection \citep{Maggiora2014}.
Notably, Quercetin exhibits higher structural similarity to DILI reference drugs yet lower network influence, reinforcing that the observed asymmetry is not driven by chemical features.

Chemical similarity analysis is an orthogonal exclusion criterion, not a risk predictor.
It rules out structural confounding; it does not establish causation.
