\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The proximity--influence paradox}

The central finding of this study is a dissociation between network metrics: proximity and influence yield inconsistent rankings.
At the primary threshold ($\geq$900), Quercetin exhibits closer proximity to DILI genes yet weaker network influence than Hyperforin; at lower thresholds ($\geq$700), this proximity ranking reverses, while influence rankings remain stable.
This instability indicates that proximity-based rankings are sensitive to network construction parameters.
Proximity measures reachability; influence measures propagation.
These are distinct topological properties; proximity is appropriate for establishing network context, but treating it as an inferential surrogate for functional impact conflates description with inference.

The proximity ranking reversal has a precise statistical explanation.
Z-scores are computed relative to degree-matched null distributions, which differ in shape depending on target set size.
Hyperforin's 10 targets yield a null distribution with standard deviation $\sigma_{null} \approx 0.16$ (700) or $0.24$ (900), whereas Quercetin's 62 targets yield tighter distributions ($\sigma_{null} \approx 0.07$ or $0.09$).
A compound with more targets has a more constrained null (law of large numbers), so equivalent deviations in observed distance translate to different Z-scores.
At $\geq$700, Hyperforin's raw distance (0.60 vs 1.34) is sufficiently smaller than Quercetin's to overcome this effect; at $\geq$900, the gap narrows (1.30 vs 1.68) and the denominator effect dominates.

Critically, this confounding does \textit{not} apply to influence-based metrics.
For RWR, Hyperforin's null distribution is \textit{wider} ($\sigma_{null} \approx 0.0097$) than Quercetin's ($\sigma_{null} \approx 0.0040$)---the opposite pattern.
A wider denominator makes it \textit{harder} to achieve large Z-scores, not easier.
Hyperforin's Z = +10.27 is therefore a conservative estimate; if null distribution widths were equalized, the advantage would be even larger.
This asymmetry---where proximity Z-scores are confounded by target count but influence Z-scores are not---indicates that influence metrics are less susceptible to sample-size artifacts.

The mechanistic explanation is equally important.
Shortest-path distance measures only minimum reachability: it returns the length of the single shortest path between a target and a disease gene, ignoring all other paths and treating all edges equally.
Random walk with restart, by contrast, integrates over \textit{all} paths weighted by transition probability, capturing how perturbation signals propagate, amplify through hubs, and attenuate through bottlenecks.
Hyperforin's targets---though fewer and more distant by shortest-path---occupy positions (PXR, CYPs, AKT1) that enable efficient propagation to the DILI module.
Quercetin's targets, though numerous and close by shortest-path, lie on peripheral or redundant paths that dilute propagated influence.

In summary, proximity was never designed as an inferential metric---it describes network topology.
However, in practice it is widely used for prioritization, implicitly treating closer proximity as higher risk.
Our results demonstrate why this use is problematic: (1) statistically, proximity Z-scores are confounded by target-count-dependent null distributions; (2) mechanistically, proximity measures reachability rather than propagation magnitude.
Influence-based methods such as RWR are designed for inference and avoid both pitfalls.

\subsection{Expression weighting refines but does not rescue proximity}

Expression-weighted influence constrains propagation to liver-active proteins.
Under this constraint, Quercetin gains influence (driven by highly expressed targets such as CFB), while Hyperforin's advantage narrows.
Crucially, the ranking is preserved: Hyperforin still exceeds Quercetin by 3.5 Z-score units.

This result has methodological implications.
If the signal had disappeared under expression weighting, one might attribute it to artifact.
Persistence under constraint provides positive evidence that the topology-only signal has biological relevance.
Attenuation does not constitute invalidation; it indicates appropriate refinement.

\subsection{Per-target network influence resolves the target-count paradox}

PTNI reframes polypharmacology as an efficiency problem rather than a coverage problem.
Compounds with fewer targets may exert greater per-target influence if those targets occupy topologically central positions.
The 17--22-fold PTNI differential between Hyperforin and Quercetin quantifies this efficiency difference.

PTNI is a normalization, not a statistical test.
It was not subjected to independent permutation testing.
Its utility lies in resolving the target-count paradox by highlighting that influence per target, not total target count, is the salient quantity.
The bootstrap analysis (Section 2.4) serves as the statistical test for this efficiency claim: Hyperforin's influence exceeds the 95\% CI of target count--matched Quercetin subsets.

Taken together, the robustness analyses provide two orthogonal protections against target-count confounding.
First, bootstrap resampling demonstrates that Hyperforin's influence exceeds the 95\% CI of target count--matched Quercetin subsets---showing the effect survives explicit size matching.
Second, null distribution analysis reveals that Hyperforin's RWR Z-score is computed against a \textit{wider} null than Quercetin's, meaning the reported Z = +10.27 is a conservative lower bound.
These independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion: the influence advantage is robust and, if anything, understated.

\subsection{Mechanistic context: the PXR--CYP axis}

The network ranking aligns with established molecular mechanisms.
Hyperforin's primary target, NR1I2 (PXR), is a master regulator of xenobiotic metabolism genes \citep{Moore2000, Watkins2001}.
PXR activation induces CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP2B6---genes directly implicated in drug-drug interactions and hepatotoxicity.
All three CYP enzymes are among Hyperforin's 10 targets in the liver-expressed network.

This architecture explains the network result: Hyperforin targets a regulatory hub that controls downstream effectors, enabling efficient signal propagation despite a small target set.
Quercetin's 62 distributed targets do not converge on a regulatory node.
High target count without hub connectivity yields diffuse, low-efficiency propagation.

\subsection{Generalizability}

The framework generalizes beyond this case study.
Any polypharmacological comparison faces the same confounds: target count, proximity, and expression context.
PTNI, expression weighting, bootstrap sensitivity, and chemical similarity controls address these systematically.
What generalizes is the analytical structure.
What does not generalize is the specific ranking, which is contingent on data sources and network construction.

\subsection{Limitations}

Drug--target databases may be incomplete; novel targets could alter the ranking.
STRING aggregates interactions across cell types; a liver-specific interactome might differ.
Network-level prioritization supports but does not prove toxicological risk; experimental validation in relevant model systems remains necessary.

\subsection{Conclusions}

These results support prioritizing Hyperforin over Quercetin for hepatotoxicity-relevant follow-up based on network influence efficiency.
The ranking is consistent with Hyperforin's established role in PXR-mediated drug interactions.
PTNI provides a principled criterion for distinguishing high-leverage perturbation from diffuse polypharmacology.

Beyond the specific case study, our results clarify the appropriate role of proximity: it provides valid descriptive context for network topology, but is threshold-dependent, confounded by null distribution shape, and yields unstable rankings when used for inference.
By restricting proximity to descriptive use and basing inference on influence metrics that survive bias checks, we provide a methodological template for computational toxicology that generalizes beyond \textit{H.~perforatum} to any setting where constituent-level attribution is required.
