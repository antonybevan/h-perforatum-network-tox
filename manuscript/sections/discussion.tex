\section{Discussion}

% Paragraph 1: Summary of key finding
Our network pharmacology analysis establishes Hyperforin as the primary hepatotoxicity liability in \textit{Hypericum perforatum} through a convergent body of evidence. 
Despite having six-fold fewer targets than Quercetin, Hyperforin achieves dramatically greater influence on DILI pathways (Z = +10.3 vs.\ +4.4), demonstrates 22-fold higher per-target efficiency, and maintains its advantage under tissue-specific expression constraints. 
Critically, this superiority cannot be explained by target-count confounding, chance target selection, or structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.

% Paragraph 2: The proximity paradox
The dissociation between proximity and influence represents a methodologically important finding. 
Shortest-path proximity---a widely used network metric---suggested Quercetin as the greater concern due to its targets' closer distance to DILI genes. 
However, proximity metrics count paths rather than weighting them by network topology. 
Random walk with restart captures the cumulative effect of multiple indirect paths, revealing that Hyperforin's targets, while fewer and more distant, occupy more topologically influential positions within the interactome.

% Paragraph 3: Biological interpretation
The biological basis of Hyperforin's mechanistic specificity likely reflects its unique target profile. 
Hyperforin potently activates PXR (NR1I2) and induces CYP2C9, CYP3A4, and ABC transporters---all central nodes in hepatic drug metabolism networks \citep{Moore2000,Watkins2003}. 
These targets are not merely abundant in liver but are topologically positioned as hubs connecting drug metabolism to broader cellular stress response pathways. 
In contrast, Quercetin's 62 targets, while individually liver-expressed, are more peripherally distributed across multiple functional modules.

% Paragraph 4: Implications for herb safety
These findings have practical implications for the safety assessment of botanical products. 
Current approaches often focus on identifying all pharmacologically active constituents without weighting their mechanistic contributions. 
Our results suggest that target count is a poor proxy for risk; a compound with few but strategically positioned targets may pose greater liability than a promiscuous compound with many weakly connected targets. 
This argues for incorporating network influence metrics into systematic safety evaluations of complex herbal extracts.

% Paragraph 5: Limitations
Several limitations warrant consideration. 
First, our analysis relies on known drug--target interactions from public databases, which may be incomplete for less-studied compounds. 
Second, the STRING interactome, while comprehensive, represents an aggregation across cell types and conditions. 
Third, PTNI is presented as a derived descriptive metric and was not formally tested via permutation; it should be interpreted as a normalization rather than an independent statistical test. 
Finally, network predictions require experimental validation in appropriate hepatotoxicity models.

% Paragraph 6: Conclusion
In conclusion, we demonstrate that Hyperforin, not Quercetin or other polyphenols, drives the hepatotoxicity signal from \textit{Hypericum perforatum} through mechanistic specificity. 
The combination of permutation-based inference, expression-weighted propagation, and multiple control analyses provides a rigorous framework that could be applied to other complex botanical products. 
Our findings support prioritizing Hyperforin content in safety-oriented quality control of St.\ John's Wort preparations.
