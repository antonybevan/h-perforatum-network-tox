\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What is ruled out}

Chemical similarity analysis excludes structural analogy to known hepatotoxins as an explanation for the network signal. 
Both compounds fall below standard analog thresholds. 
The observed influence differences therefore arise from target engagement patterns, not chemical class effects.

\subsection{What alternative explanation is eliminated}

Bootstrap resampling excludes target count as a sufficient explanation. 
No random 10-target sample from Quercetin's pool achieves Hyperforin's influence. 
The advantage is not an artefact of having fewer, ``luckier'' targets.

\subsection{Why efficiency reframes polypharmacology}

PTNI normalizes influence by target count, reframing the comparison as efficiency rather than magnitude. 
Hyperforin achieves 22-fold higher influence per target. 
This shifts the question from ``which compound has more influence?'' to ``which compound uses its targets more effectively?''

Polypharmacology is often equated with liability. 
These data indicate that target count is a poor proxy for risk. 
A compound with few, strategically positioned targets may pose greater concern than a promiscuous compound with many peripheral targets.

\subsection{Why biology constrains but does not rescue proximity}

Expression-weighted propagation tests whether topological findings persist under tissue-specific constraints. 
The gap narrows but does not reverse. 
Quercetin's high-expression targets (notably CFB) improve its signal propagation, but not enough to overcome Hyperforin's topological advantage.

This addresses a common objection: that network topology without expression context is biologically unrealistic. 
The constraint was applied. The ranking held.

\subsection{Why the field's intuition fails}

Network proximity is intuitive: closer should mean more connected. 
But proximity counts paths; it does not weight them by topology. 
RWR captures cumulative influence across multiple indirect paths.

Quercetin is closer but weaker. 
Hyperforin is farther but stronger. 
The dissociation demonstrates that shortest-path metrics---while widely used---do not predict functional influence.

\subsection{Limitations}

Drug--target interactions from public databases may be incomplete. 
The STRING interactome aggregates across cell types. 
PTNI was not formally tested via permutation. 
Network predictions require experimental validation.

\subsection{Conclusion}

Hyperforin, not Quercetin, drives the hepatotoxicity signal from \textit{Hypericum perforatum}.
The evidence is: higher influence despite fewer targets, persistence under expression constraint, exclusion of target-count and structural-analog explanations.
The mechanism is specificity, not promiscuity.
