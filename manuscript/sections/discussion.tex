\section{Discussion}

These results demonstrate that network proximity alone is insufficient for toxicological prioritization.
Quercetin, despite more targets and closer proximity to DILI genes, exerts weaker influence than Hyperforin.
Influence efficiency---not target count or path length---distinguishes high-risk compounds.

\subsection{Proximity versus influence}

The rank reversal between proximity and influence is not a methodological artifact; it reflects a conceptual distinction.
Proximity counts shortest paths.
Influence weights them by network topology, capturing the cumulative effect of multiple indirect routes \citep{Kohler2008}.
When these metrics diverge, proximity-based predictions may mislead \citep{Menche2015}.

\subsection{PTNI as a normalization principle}

Per-target network influence (PTNI) resolves the target-count paradox by reframing polypharmacology as an efficiency problem.
A compound with few, strategically positioned targets can achieve higher PTNI than one with many peripheral targets.
The 22-fold PTNI difference between Hyperforin and Quercetin quantifies this distinction.
PTNI is descriptive---it was not independently tested---but its consistency across topology-only and expression-weighted analyses supports its utility as a normalization principle.

\subsection{Biological constraint}

Expression weighting attenuates but does not reverse Hyperforin's advantage.
Quercetin gains from high-expression targets (CFB); Hyperforin's topological position remains dominant.
This addresses a common critique: that network topology without expression context lacks biological realism.
The constraint was applied; the ranking held.

\subsection{Generalizability}

This framework extends beyond \textit{H.\ perforatum}.
Any polypharmacological comparison faces the same target-count confound.
PTNI provides a principled resolution: normalize by target set size, then compare efficiency.
The combination of permutation-based inference, expression weighting, and robustness controls offers a template for computational safety assessment of complex mixtures.

\subsection{Limitations}

Drug--target databases may be incomplete; novel targets could alter the analysis.
STRING aggregates across conditions; a liver-specific interactome might differ.
PTNI is a normalization, not a formal statistical test.
Network predictions require experimental validation.
Extension to other \textit{H.\ perforatum} constituents would strengthen generalizability.

\subsection{Conclusions}

Hyperforin drives the hepatotoxicity signal from \textit{Hypericum perforatum} through mechanistic specificity, not target promiscuity.
Influence efficiency, not target count or proximity, predicts risk.
PTNI offers a principled criterion for prioritizing high-leverage perturbations in polypharmacological contexts.
