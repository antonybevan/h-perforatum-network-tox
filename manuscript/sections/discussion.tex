\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The proximity--influence paradox}

The central finding of this study is a dissociation between network metrics: proximity and influence yield inconsistent rankings.
At the primary threshold ($\geq$900), Quercetin exhibits closer proximity to DILI genes yet weaker network influence than Hyperforin; at lower thresholds ($\geq$700), this proximity ranking reverses, while influence rankings remain stable.
This instability challenges a common heuristic in network medicine---that targets closer to disease genes pose greater risk.
Proximity measures reachability; influence measures propagation.
These are distinct topological properties, and conflating them leads to systematic misattribution.

The paradox arises because shortest-path distance treats all paths equally, ignoring how signals amplify or attenuate through hub nodes.
Hyperforin's targets, though fewer, occupy positions that enable efficient propagation to the DILI module.
Quercetin's targets, though numerous and close, lie on peripheral or redundant paths.

\subsection{Expression weighting refines but does not rescue proximity}

Expression-weighted influence constrains propagation to liver-active proteins.
Under this constraint, Quercetin gains influence (driven by highly expressed targets such as CFB), while Hyperforin's advantage narrows.
Crucially, the ranking is preserved: Hyperforin still exceeds Quercetin by 3.5 Z-score units.

This result has methodological implications.
If the signal had disappeared under expression weighting, one might attribute it to artifact.
Persistence under constraint provides positive evidence that the topology-only signal has biological relevance.
Attenuation does not constitute invalidation; it indicates appropriate refinement.

\subsection{Per-target network influence resolves the target-count paradox}

PTNI reframes polypharmacology as an efficiency problem rather than a coverage problem.
Compounds with fewer targets may exert greater per-target influence if those targets occupy topologically central positions.
The 17--22-fold PTNI differential between Hyperforin and Quercetin quantifies this efficiency difference.

PTNI is a normalization, not a statistical test.
It was not subjected to independent permutation testing.
Its utility lies in resolving the target-count paradox by highlighting that influence per target, not total target count, is the salient quantity.

\subsection{Mechanistic context: the PXR--CYP axis}

The network ranking aligns with established molecular mechanisms.
Hyperforin's primary target, NR1I2 (PXR), is a master regulator of xenobiotic metabolism genes \citep{Moore2000, Watkins2001}.
PXR activation induces CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP2B6---genes directly implicated in drug-drug interactions and hepatotoxicity.
All three CYP enzymes are among Hyperforin's 10 targets in the liver-expressed network.

This architecture explains the network result: Hyperforin targets a regulatory hub that controls downstream effectors, enabling efficient signal propagation despite a small target set.
Quercetin's 62 distributed targets do not converge on a regulatory node.
High target count without hub connectivity yields diffuse, low-efficiency propagation.

\subsection{Generalizability}

The framework generalizes beyond this case study.
Any polypharmacological comparison faces the same confounds: target count, proximity, and expression context.
PTNI, expression weighting, bootstrap sensitivity, and chemical similarity controls address these systematically.
What generalizes is the analytical structure.
What does not generalize is the specific ranking, which is contingent on data sources and network construction.

\subsection{Limitations}

Drug--target databases may be incomplete; novel targets could alter the ranking.
STRING aggregates interactions across cell types; a liver-specific interactome might differ.
PTNI is a normalization, not a statistical test.
Network-level prioritization supports but does not prove toxicological risk.
Experimental validation in relevant model systems remains necessary.

\subsection{Exclusion controls}

Bootstrap resampling eliminates target count as a sufficient explanation for the ranking.
No random 10-target subset from Quercetin's pool matches Hyperforin's observed influence.
Chemical similarity analysis excludes structural resemblance to known hepatotoxins as a confound.
Both compounds fall below the 0.4 Tanimoto threshold.
These controls strengthen confidence in the ranking without adding independent evidence.

\subsection{Conclusions}

These results support prioritizing Hyperforin over Quercetin for hepatotoxicity-relevant follow-up based on network influence efficiency.
The ranking is consistent with Hyperforin's established role in PXR-mediated drug interactions.
PTNI provides a principled criterion for distinguishing high-leverage perturbation from diffuse polypharmacology.
This framework is applicable to other complex botanical extracts where constituent-level attribution is required.
