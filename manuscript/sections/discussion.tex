\section{Discussion}

Our network pharmacology analysis provides convergent evidence that Hyperforin, not Quercetin, is the primary hepatotoxicity liability in \textit{Hypericum perforatum}.
Despite having six-fold fewer targets, Hyperforin achieves dramatically greater influence on DILI pathways, demonstrates 22-fold higher per-target efficiency, and maintains its advantage under tissue-specific expression constraints.
Critically, this superiority cannot be explained by target-count confounding, chance target selection, or structural analogy to known hepatotoxins.

\subsection{The proximity paradox}

The dissociation between proximity and influence represents a methodologically important finding with implications beyond the present case study.
Shortest-path proximity---a widely used metric in network medicine \citep{Menche2015, Guney2016}---suggested Quercetin as the greater concern due to its targets' closer topological distance to DILI genes.
However, proximity metrics count paths rather than weighting them by network structure.

Random walk with restart captures the cumulative effect of multiple indirect paths, effectively measuring how much signal arrives at disease genes from compound targets after equilibrating through the network \citep{Kohler2008}.
The rank reversal we observe (closer but weaker vs.\ farther but stronger) demonstrates that proximity and influence measure fundamentally different network properties.
This finding argues for caution in interpreting proximity-based predictions without complementary influence analysis.

\subsection{Biological basis of mechanistic specificity}

The biological basis of Hyperforin's superior efficiency likely reflects its unique target profile.
Hyperforin potently activates PXR (NR1I2), the master regulator of xenobiotic response, leading to coordinated induction of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and ABC transporters \citep{Moore2000, Watkins2001}.
These targets are not merely abundant in liver tissue but occupy topologically central positions connecting drug metabolism to broader cellular stress response pathways \citep{Klaassen2010}.

In contrast, Quercetin's 62 targets, while individually liver-expressed, are distributed across multiple functional modules: flavonoid biosynthesis, cell signaling, and antioxidant response \citep{Boots2008}.
This dispersion dilutes the compound's influence on any single pathway, including DILI-relevant pathways.
The 22-fold PTNI difference quantifies this distinction: Hyperforin's targets are ``focused'' on hepatotoxicity-relevant nodes, while Quercetin's are ``scattered.''

\subsection{Expression weighting as biological constraint}

Expression-weighted influence propagation tests whether topological findings persist under biologically realistic constraints.
By weighting edge transitions by liver gene expression, we preferentially route signal through tissue-relevant portions of the interactome.
The observation that Hyperforin's advantage narrows ($\Delta$Z: +5.8 $\rightarrow$ +3.5) but does not reverse under this constraint is informative.

The narrowing reflects the fact that some of Quercetin's targets (notably CFB at 1,115 TPM) are highly expressed in liver, boosting its signal propagation when expression is considered.
However, Hyperforin's core advantage---deriving from topological position rather than expression level---remains robust.
This addresses a common critique of network analyses: that topology without expression context is biologically unrealistic.
The constraint was applied; the ranking held.

\subsection{Implications for botanical safety assessment}

These findings have practical implications for safety evaluation of complex botanical products.
Current approaches often focus on enumerating pharmacologically active constituents without weighting their mechanistic contributions \citep{Heinrich2020}.
Our results demonstrate that target count is a poor proxy for risk: a compound with few strategically positioned targets may pose greater liability than a promiscuous compound with many peripheral targets.

For \textit{H.\ perforatum} specifically, these data support prioritizing Hyperforin content in safety-oriented quality control.
This is consistent with the observation that drug interaction liability correlates with Hyperforin content \citep{Bauer2004}.
Formulations deliberately depleted of Hyperforin (e.g., Ze 117) may offer improved safety profiles, though this remains to be validated for hepatotoxicity specifically.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant consideration.
First, drug--target interactions from public databases (STITCH, ChEMBL) may be incomplete for less-studied compounds; novel targets could alter the analysis.
Second, the STRING interactome aggregates interactions across cell types and conditions; a liver-specific interactome might yield different results.
Third, PTNI is presented as a derived descriptive metric without independent statistical testing; it should be interpreted as a normalization rather than a formal test.
Fourth, network predictions, while mechanistically grounded, require experimental validation in appropriate hepatotoxicity models.
Fifth, our analysis focuses on two compounds; extension to other \textit{H.\ perforatum} constituents (hypericin, Rutin) would strengthen generalizability.

\subsection{Conclusions}

We demonstrate that Hyperforin, through mechanistic specificity rather than target promiscuity, drives the network-based hepatotoxicity signal from \textit{Hypericum perforatum}.
The evidence includes: (1) higher influence despite fewer and more distant targets; (2) persistence under expression-weighted constraint; (3) 22-fold efficiency advantage; (4) exclusion of target-count and structural-analog confounders.
The combination of permutation-based inference, expression-weighted propagation, and multiple control analyses provides a rigorous framework applicable to other complex botanical products.
These findings support the regulatory focus on Hyperforin content for safety evaluation of St.\ John's Wort preparations.
