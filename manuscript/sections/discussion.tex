\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Chemical similarity control}

Chemical similarity analysis excludes the possibility that either compound is a structural analog of known hepatotoxins.
Maximum Tanimoto similarity to DILIrank reference drugs falls below the 0.4 threshold for both compounds.
This rules out structural resemblance as an alternative explanation for the observed network signal.
It does not imply that either compound is structurally inert.

\subsection{Bootstrap robustness}

Bootstrap resampling eliminates target-count as a sufficient explanation for Hyperforin's ranking.
No random 10-target subset from Quercetin's pool matches Hyperforin's observed influence.
This strengthens confidence in the ranking without adding independent evidence.
The bootstrap is a robustness control, not a confirmatory test.

\subsection{Per-target network influence}

PTNI reframes polypharmacology as an efficiency problem rather than a coverage problem.
Compounds with fewer targets may exert higher per-target influence if those targets occupy topologically central positions.
PTNI is a conceptual normalization; it was not subjected to permutation testing and should not be interpreted as a hypothesis test.
Its utility lies in resolving the target-count paradox, not in providing additional inference.

\subsection{Expression-weighted influence}

Expression weighting modulates but does not rescue proximity-based intuition.
Quercetin gains influence under liver-specific expression constraints; Hyperforin's advantage narrows but persists.
Attenuation is expected when expression reweights signal propagation.
Attenuation does not constitute invalidation.

\subsection{The proximity--influence paradox}

Proximity counts shortest paths; influence weights them by topology.
These are distinct properties.
The rank reversal observed here---closer proximity, weaker influence---challenges a common heuristic in network medicine: that targets closer to disease genes are more relevant.
This assumption is convenient but incomplete.
Influence-based metrics, while computationally more demanding, are not reducible to distance.

\subsection{Generalizability}

The framework generalizes beyond this case study.
Any polypharmacological comparison faces the same confounds: target count, proximity, and expression context.
PTNI, expression weighting, and robustness controls address these systematically.
The framework is a template, not a validation of Hyperforin hepatotoxicity.
What generalizes: the analytical structure.
What does not generalize: the specific ranking, which is contingent on data sources and network construction.

\subsection{Limitations}

Drug--target databases may be incomplete; novel targets could alter the ranking.
STRING aggregates interactions across cell types; a liver-specific interactome might differ.
PTNI is a normalization, not a statistical test.
Network-level prioritization supports but does not prove toxicological risk.
Experimental validation remains necessary.

\subsection{Mechanistic context: the PXR--CYP axis}

The network ranking is consistent with established molecular mechanisms.
Hyperforin's primary target, NR1I2 (PXR, pregnane X receptor), is a master regulator of xenobiotic metabolism genes (Table~\ref{tab:S1}).
PXR activation induces transcription of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP2B6---genes directly implicated in drug-drug interactions and hepatotoxicity \citep{Moore2000, Watkins2001}.
All three CYP enzymes are among Hyperforin's 10 LCC targets.
This pathway architecture explains why a compound with few targets exerts disproportionate network influence: it targets a regulatory hub that controls downstream effectors.

Quercetin, by contrast, engages 62 distributed targets including kinases (SRC, AKT1, GSK3B), transporters (ABCG2, ABCC1), and metabolic enzymes (CYP1A2, ALDH2).
These targets are topologically peripheral and do not converge on a common regulatory node.
High target count without hub connectivity yields diffuse, low-efficiency propagation.

The mechanistic distinction---master-regulator strategy versus diffuse polypharmacology---is quantified by PTNI.
PTNI does not identify the mechanism; it provides a metric that distinguishes compounds where mechanism identification is warranted.

\subsection{Conclusions}

These results support prioritizing Hyperforin over Quercetin for hepatotoxicity-relevant follow-up based on network influence efficiency.
The ranking is consistent with Hyperforin's established role in PXR-mediated drug interactions.
PTNI provides a principled criterion for distinguishing high-leverage perturbation from diffuse polypharmacology.
This framework is applicable to other complex botanical extracts where constituent-level attribution is required.
